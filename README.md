### What ENSv2 Changes About Subdomain Leasing

#### The Story: Why ENSv2 Stays on Layer 1

When ENS first announced ENSv2 in late 2024, the plan was to move to a dedicated Layer 2 chain called **Namechain** â€” purpose-built to handle the gas costs of registering and managing millions of .eth names. At the time, registering an ENS name on Ethereum mainnet could cost nearly $5 in gas fees. Building a separate L2 seemed like the only way to make subdomain leasing economically viable at scale.

That calculus changed completely in 2025.

Ethereum's Fusaka upgrade doubled the gas limit from 30 million to 60 million â€” and core developers are now targeting 200 million by 2026. The result: <strong>ENS registration gas costs fell 99% in a single year</strong>, dropping from ~$5 to under $0.05 per name. ENS lead developer nick.eth ran the numbers and found that subsidizing every ENS transaction in all of 2025 would cost roughly $10,000 at current prices â€” and even under peak post-Fusaka congestion, just $250,000. Running a dedicated L2 like Namechain would cost substantially more than that.

On February 6, 2026, ENS officially announced: **Namechain is canceled. ENSv2 deploys exclusively on Ethereum Layer 1.**

> *"Ethereum L1 is scaling, and it's scaling faster than almost anyone predicted two years ago. By staying on L1, we're aligning ENS with the strongest possible infrastructure guarantees â€” Ethereum itself."*
> â€” nick.eth, ENS Lead Developer

This is not a retreat. It is a strategic upgrade. ENS co-founder Nick Johnson noted that ENSv2 was designed from the beginning to work on either L1 or L2 â€” so the core architecture, the new registry system, improved ownership models, and better name expiration handling all ship as planned. The only thing that changed is where it runs.

#### What This Means for the ENS Ecosystem

**For users:** No more bridging. No separate wallet. No cross-chain complexity. Everything happens on Ethereum â€” the chain users already know, with the security model they already trust.

**For L2s:** ENSv2 still supports resolution across 60+ blockchains including Bitcoin and Solana. The new registration flow abstracts cross-chain complexity, so you can register a .eth name from any EVM chain without manual bridging. ENS stays multi-chain compatible â€” it just lives on L1.

**For the broader Ethereum narrative:** This is a signal. When one of Web3's most widely adopted protocols with 2.2+ million registered names chooses L1 over its own L2, it tells the market that Ethereum's base layer is becoming viable for applications that were previously priced out. Nick Johnson also predicted the L2 space itself will contract â€” splitting between commodity rollups competing on fees and more specialized chains with fundamentally different execution models.
